Gender and potential factors in relation to unpaid care

Kunhao Li*,
University of Sheffield
*Indicates Equal Contribution
Code

Abstract

Background: the increasing of ageing population drives more attention on unpaid care work and persistent gender differences in providing unpaid care work were still discussed around world. Aim: This paper aimed to examine whether there were gender differences in splitting the unpaid care work and the potential factors that were associated with the duration of providing unpaid care using UK census data. Methods: UK 2021 and 2011 census data was used. Chi-square tests were used to analyse the association between provision of unpaid care work and other four variables (ages, gender, education level and employment status), and logistic regression model was developed accordingly. Results: Significant associations were found between unpaid care work and those four covariates. Conclusion: Studying the provision of unpaid care work can be beneficial for both the individual and the society to pay more attention to the well-beings and future developments.

Research Questions

Research question 1: Is there any gender difference in providing unpaid care work in the UK?

Research question 2: What are potential factors associated with hours of providing unpaid care?

Key Findings

1. There was significant association between gender and hours of unpaid care work in the UK;

2. By comparing the UK census data in 2011 and 2021, both male and female had an increasing trend in providing longer unpaid care work;

3. Ages, gender, employment status and education level were statistically significantly related to the durations of unpaid care work by evaluating the UK 2021 census data. People who were older, female, with lower level of educational background and not in the employment were more likely to provide longer unpaid care work per week.
